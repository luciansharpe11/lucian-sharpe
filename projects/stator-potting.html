Case Study 01

Motor Stator Potting Process Development

Developed an in-house vacuum stator potting process for high-power electric motors, including custom tooling and internal prototype manufacturing capability. Achieved approximately 80-90% fill rates supporting 500+ hour endurance testing.

Short Summary

This work established an internal process for vacuum potting motor stators when existing manufacturing guidance and internal process history were limited. The effort covered material selection, tooling, equipment validation, and iteration toward repeatable fill performance.

The Problem

Thermal performance depended on consistent resin fill in a geometry that was difficult to wet out and vulnerable to trapped air. The team needed a prototype-capable internal process rather than relying on slow external iteration.

Constraints

  • High-viscosity resin and significant air entrapment risk
  • Limited internal process history for this application
  • Prototype equipment limitations and evolving tooling concepts
  • Need to move quickly enough to support motor endurance testing

My Approach

  • Researched candidate materials and worked with resin vendors on process windows
  • Designed custom tooling to improve fixture control and repeatability
  • Built a vacuum potting setup for internal prototype use
  • Ran iterative trials to study fill behavior, defects, and practical operating limits

What I Built

  • Internal vacuum potting equipment for prototype production
  • Custom tooling concepts to support part handling and process control
  • Defined process parameters and a scale-up path toward automated production

Results

  • Established internal prototype capability for high-power stator potting
  • Achieved approximately 80-90% fill rates in development testing
  • Supported 500+ hour endurance testing with stable thermal performance

What Went Wrong / Iterations

Early iterations exposed how sensitive the process was to trapped air, resin handling, and tooling assumptions. Several setup details that looked acceptable on paper produced uneven fill in practice, so the process had to be simplified and tightened around what could be controlled reliably on internal equipment.

What I'd Do Next

The next step would be a more formal design of experiments around fill performance, defect rate, and cycle time, followed by a production-ready transfer into automated equipment.
